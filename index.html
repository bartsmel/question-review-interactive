Got It Right ‚Äî Quick Confidence Check

You don't need to linger here, but a brief intentional review keeps knowledge sharp and builds fluency, not just familiarity.

1
Read the educational objective
Read

Every question has a teaching point. Read it ‚Äî even briefly ‚Äî to confirm you understood what was being tested, not just what led you to the right answer.

Why this mattersSometimes you get the right answer for the right reasons; sometimes you get lucky with partial understanding. The objective helps you tell the difference.
2
Confirm your reasoning ‚Äî then move on
Reflect

Ask yourself: Did I get this right for the right reasons? If yes ‚Äî trust yourself. If something felt uncertain even as you got it right, note it mentally for a light review next session.

There's no need to dwell. Your time is better spent on genuine gaps. Move to the next question.

Flagged / Guessed Right ‚Äî Find the Knowledge Crack

You got credit, but you weren't fully sure. There's a learning opportunity hiding in this "correct" answer ‚Äî don't let it slip by.

1
Read the correct answer and its full explanation
Read

Read the explanation carefully ‚Äî even though you got it right. You're looking for the specific detail or reasoning that would have made you confident, not just correct.

2
Identify the knowledge crack
Diagnose

Ask yourself: What specific detail or connection was I missing that kept me from feeling certain?

Knowledge Crack vs. Knowledge GapA crack means you know the topic but are fuzzy on a detail. A gap means the concept is largely unfamiliar. Cracks need reinforcement; gaps need rebuilding.
3
Record the missing detail
Record

Write the specific detail in First Aid, a notebook, or a flashcard. Be targeted ‚Äî not "review murmurs" but the exact distinguishing fact that tripped you up.

Example"Mitral regurgitation ‚Üí holosystolic murmur radiating to axilla, louder with handgrip."
4
Summarize in your own words
Recall

In 1‚Äì2 sentences, explain why the right answer is right ‚Äî in your own words. This is active recall: your brain remembers what it has to generate, not what it passively reads.

  • The correct answer is ___ because ___.
  • The key feature that distinguishes this is ___.

Got It Wrong ‚Äî Deep Review

This is where the real learning happens. Getting a question wrong is a gift ‚Äî it's showing you exactly where to focus. The goal isn't to feel bad; it's to get curious and then get busy.

1
Before reading ‚Äî reconstruct your reasoning
Before you read

Before you look at the explanation, write out or think through: Why did I pick what I picked?

  • I chose ___ because I was thinking ___.
  • The part of the stem that threw me off was ___.
Why before reading?If you read the explanation first, your brain will retroactively fit your memory of your reasoning to the correct answer ‚Äî making it harder to identify what actually went wrong.
2
Revisit the stem carefully
Re-read

Read the question stem again. Did you miss a qualifier, misread the task, or overlook a key detail? Check specifically: patient age/sex/setting, time course language (acute vs. chronic), and what the question is actually asking (diagnosis? next step? mechanism?).

3
Engage with content (for knowledge gaps)
Study

If this is a knowledge gap, look it up in a primary resource ‚Äî don't just re-read the question explanation. You want to understand the surrounding framework, not just the isolated fact. Understanding how the piece fits the bigger picture is what makes knowledge sticky.

Not a knowledge gap?If you already understood the content, skip the resource dive ‚Äî head to error analysis first to figure out what kind of error this actually was.
4
Write your 1‚Äì2 sentence summary
Recall

In your own words, write it out. This is the most important step for retention ‚Äî your brain remembers what it has to generate, not what it passively reads.

  • I incorrectly chose ___ because ___. This was wrong because ___.
  • The correct answer is ___ because ___. The key distinguishing feature was ___.
5
Record for spaced review
Record

Add this to your active review materials ‚Äî a flashcard, First Aid annotation, or mistake log. Getting it wrong once is learning; forgetting it and getting it wrong again is wasted effort.

What to recordThe concept, the key distinguishing feature, and ideally a brief clinical pearl that will trigger the right thinking next time.

Error Analysis ‚Äî Why Did I Miss This?

Before you can fix an error, you need to understand what kind of error it is. Each type calls for a different response ‚Äî matching your remedy to your error type is what makes review efficient.

1
Identify your error type
Diagnose

Read each category below. Which one best describes what happened on this question?

üß† Knowledge Gap "I didn't know this" / "I've never seen this topic." Fix: Go to a primary resource, understand the framework, create a flashcard. Ask how this fits what you know about the system.
üí° Reasoning Error "I knew it but applied it wrong" / "I second-guessed myself" / "I confused this with something else." Fix: Trace your logic ‚Äî where did it derail? A comparison note (e.g., Crohn's vs. UC differences) is often more valuable than reviewing either topic alone.
üéØ Test-Taking Issue "I misread NOT/EXCEPT" / "I changed my correct answer" / "Time pressure." Fix: This is a strategy issue. Circle negatives in every question. Commit to first instinct unless you find a specific reason to change. Build pacing stamina with timed blocks.

  • I confused ___ with ___. The difference is ___.

Spaced Review ‚Äî Turning Today's Work into Long-Term Memory

Reviewing a missed concept once is good. Reviewing it at the right intervals is what actually moves it into long-term memory.

üìê Why spacing works

The spacing effect shows that information reviewed at increasing intervals is retained far better than information reviewed all at once. Reviewing a concept when you're just about to forget it is when memory consolidation is strongest.

Doing 100 questions and reviewing them all once is far less effective than reviewing wrong answers again tomorrow, in 3 days, then in a week.

üìì Keep a mistake log

A mistake log is a running document where you record missed concepts along with your 1‚Äì2 sentence summary. It doesn't need to be an app ‚Äî a Google Doc, notebook, or First Aid annotations all work.

Key habit: Spend 5‚Äì10 minutes at the start of each study session reading recent entries from your mistake log before opening new questions. Low effort, high return.
